📊 1. Structural Modeling & Class Diagrams

Static view of the system — classes, attributes, operations, and relationships

📖 Explain

Structural Modeling captures the static features of a system: the objects, their types, and how they are related. The most common structural model is the Class Diagram, which shows classes, interfaces, and relationships like association, aggregation, and generalization.

  • Class Diagram = blueprint of the system's structure.
  • Does NOT show behavior over time (that is dynamic modeling).
  • Essential for communicating design between stakeholders.

🔗 2. Association Relationship

Structural connection between classes — multiplicity, roles, and navigation

📖 Explain

Association is a relationship where objects of one class are connected to objects of another class to communicate or exchange data. It can have:

  • Name: e.g., "works-for"
  • Multiplicity: defines how many instances are linked (1, 0..*, 1..*, etc.)
  • Role: optional role names
🏫 Example: School 1..* works-for 1 University
Many schools can be associated with one university.

🧩 3. Aggregation Relationship

Special association: "whole-part" hierarchy (hollow diamond)

📖 Explain

Aggregation is a specialized form of association representing a whole-part relationship. The "whole" contains the "parts", but parts can exist independently. Graphically: a line with a hollow diamond attached to the whole class.

  • Example: University (whole) contains Schools (parts).
  • Parts can survive without the whole (weak containment).

📝 Exam-Style Questions & Answers (Check Your Progress)

Q1. What is a Class Diagram?
Answer: A Class Diagram is a structural UML diagram that depicts the logical structure of an object-oriented system. It provides a static view by illustrating classes, interfaces, attributes, operations, and relationships such as association, aggregation, generalization, and dependency. It serves as a blueprint for system design and is widely used in analysis and design phases.
Q2. Define Association in UML. How is it graphically represented?
Answer: Association is a structural relationship indicating that objects of one class are connected to objects of another class, allowing them to communicate or exchange data. Graphically, it is represented as a solid line connecting two classes, often accompanied by multiplicity (e.g., 1, 0..*), role names, and an optional association name. For example, a line between Student and Course with multiplicity "1..*" and name "enrolls-in".
Q3. What is "Multiplicity" in an association?
Answer: Multiplicity defines the number of instances of one class that can be linked to a single instance of the other class. Common notations: 1 (exactly one), 0..1 (zero or one), 0..* (zero or many), 1..* (one or many). For instance, in a University and School association, multiplicity "1..*" on the School side means one University can have many Schools.
Q4. Explain Aggregation with the help of a suitable example.
Answer: Aggregation is a specialized form of association that represents a "whole-part" hierarchy. It is denoted by a solid line with a hollow diamond attached to the whole class. For example, a Car (whole) aggregates Wheel (parts). Wheels can be created independently and still belong to the car, but the relationship indicates composition of parts. Another example: University (whole) and School (part). The hollow diamond emphasizes that the part can exist without the whole.
Q5. How does a normal Association differ from Aggregation?
Answer: A normal Association is a generic connection between classes where both classes are conceptually independent (e.g., Customer and Order). Aggregation is a stronger form implying a "whole-part" relationship with a clear containment (e.g., Team and Player). Aggregation uses a hollow diamond at the whole side, while association uses a plain line. Moreover, aggregation suggests that the part belongs to the whole, but the part can survive the whole's destruction (weak containment).
